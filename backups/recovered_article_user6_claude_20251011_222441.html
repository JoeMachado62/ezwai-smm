Tariffs and AI Are Rewriting the Rules of U.S. Auto Retail

How trade policy and artificial intelligence are transforming dealership operations and consumer car buying

AUTO INDUSTRY SPECIAL • 2025

Tariffs and Trade: Pricing, Supply, and Policy

The U.S. automotive retail sector is confronting a two-front transformation in 2025: tariff-driven cost pressures across global supply chains and the rapid infusion of artificial intelligence into pricing, inventory management, and customer engagement. Together, these forces are reshaping sticker prices, dealer margins, and the way consumers shop for new and used vehicles.

On the trade front, multiple tariff regimes now overlap in the U.S. market. A longstanding 25% tariff on imported light trucks dating to the 1960s remains in force (often called the "chicken tax"). Since 2018, Section 301 measures have imposed tariff rates—commonly 25%—on many Chinese-origin automotive parts and select vehicles. In May 2024, the administration announced further tariff adjustments, including raising the tariff on Chinese-made electric vehicles to 100%.

"Tariffs have introduced a layer of complexity and cost that dealers must navigate carefully, but AI offers a pathway to greater efficiency."

Economic research on the 2018–2019 tariff waves found that most tariff costs were borne by U.S. importers and, in many cases, passed through to downstream buyers. In auto retail specifically, higher input costs can influence vehicle pricing and reconditioning expenses for used inventory. Average new-vehicle transaction prices hovered around the upper-$40,000s through 2024, reflecting not only tariffs but also shifts in model mix, features, and financing conditions since the pandemic.

Supply chains have continued to adapt. Automakers and tiered suppliers have diversified sourcing to Mexico, elsewhere in North America, and parts of Southeast Asia to mitigate tariff exposure and logistics risks. Franchised dealers report that new-vehicle inventory levels have generally improved from their pandemic lows, but parts availability—particularly for electronics and advanced driver-assistance components—remains uneven.

AI in the Dealership

Artificial intelligence is increasingly embedded in dealership operations, from live market pricing to appraisal guidance and inventory turn optimization. Dealers and third-party platforms use models trained on historical transactions, regional demand, and vehicle condition data to fine-tune list prices and acquisition bids.

Digital retailing has also matured since 2020. Industry surveys by automotive consultancies and trade groups report that more dealers now enable shoppers to complete key steps online—credit pre-approval, trade-in valuation, and even remote delivery—before visiting the showroom.

AI Implementation Challenges

Budget and integration hurdles persist. Multi-store groups often consolidate data across CRM, DMS, and pricing systems to unlock AI's benefits, while single-point stores face steeper per-store costs and technical complexity.

Generative AI and conversational systems are emerging in lead qualification, service scheduling, and customer support. Dealers report time savings in handling routine inquiries and improved responsiveness during peak traffic hours. Still, store operators and compliance specialists caution that human oversight remains essential for accuracy, privacy, and fair-lending safeguards.

"While AI tools are promising, the upfront investment and integration complexity can be barriers for smaller dealers."

Evidence from dealer surveys and vendor case studies suggests AI-enabled pricing and inventory tools can reduce days-to-turn and improve gross margins in specific segments, particularly late-model used vehicles where market comparables are plentiful.

Winners, Strugglers, and the Consumer

Larger dealer groups with centralized data teams and access to capital are generally better positioned to absorb tariff-related cost increases and deploy AI at scale. These groups can negotiate supplier terms, re-route parts procurement, and invest in model training and governance. Smaller independent stores, particularly those competing on price in local used markets, face tighter margins.

Affordability remains a central headwind for consumers. Elevated interest rates through 2024 and early 2025 increased monthly payments, even as incentives and inventory improved in some segments. Analysts note a renewed consumer shift toward used vehicles and certified pre-owned models when new-vehicle payments exceed budget thresholds.

Real Dealership Results

CarMax: Refining appraisal models and leveraging online channels for efficient buyer-vehicle matching amid parts-cost volatility.

Lithia Motors: Using centralized analytics for acquisition and pricing strategies across growing store footprint.

Asbury Automotive: Clicklane platform speeds deal structuring with AI-enabled workflows.

Consumer expectations have moved decisively toward omnichannel buying. J.D. Power's retail studies have found higher satisfaction among buyers who complete more steps online while still having access to in-person assistance for test drives and F&I products.

"The next year will test dealer operational agility as they hedge inventory and negotiate alternative parts sources."

Manufacturers and suppliers are localizing production where possible. North American investment in EV components and batteries has accelerated under the Inflation Reduction Act's consumer tax-credit rules and domestic content incentives, while USMCA maintains regional value-content thresholds that shape sourcing decisions.

Key Takeaways

Technology Adoption: Data discipline and process adoption determine AI success, particularly in used vehicle segments.

Market Positioning: Scale benefits in procurement and technology sustain margins across economic cycles.

Consumer Behavior: Shift toward omnichannel buying experiences combining online convenience with in-person service.
